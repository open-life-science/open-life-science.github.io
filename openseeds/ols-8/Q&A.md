## Cohort calls
16. What platform is used for cohort calls? 
    * Zoom. No call platform is perfect, but we chose Zoom because it offers breakout room facilities, good host/muting controls, and easy integration with captioning services - essential to ensure inclusion. It also works better with low-bandwidth connections and lower-powered computers, compared to some of the other platforms available.  
17. How do I access the cohort calls? 
    * Every week we’ll send out an email (a mailing via Civi) with a link to the “pad” (shared call notes). The cohort call link is in there. The pad link can also be found in the OLS calendar. 
18. Do I need to show my face during cohort calls? 
    * Not at all! If you’d like to, you’re welcome to, but there’s no obligation to share your face :) 
19. Do I need to speak during cohort calls? 
    * This is not required. You can interact via the chat and etherpad in the main call room, and in breakout rooms, we offer the choice of written or spoken breakouts. 
20. How do I find the cohort call notes or also called “etherpads” or “pads”. 
    * Check your email for the weekly email (check spam if you don’t get one), and/or look at the OLS calendar
21. What happens if I can’t join the cohort calls?
    * It’s important to join some if you can, but if you can’t, we upload them to YouTube a few days after the call. Try to come along to co-working instead (on Fridays), if you can’t make it to cohort calls. 
22. What roles can I see in OLS? 
    * Expert, speaker, mentor, mentee (aka project lead), facilitator (see more info in “roles” below). 
23. Can I add new project members? 
    * Yes, but there needs to be a strong justification for it and you have to explain that to us, sending an email to team@we-are-ols.org

## Accessibility 
24. If I am blind, can I still join the calls? 
    * Please do! We use etherpad for notes based on the advice that it is better for visual impairment and screen readers than google docs, and we try to routinely alt-text our images. We welcome requests for other kinds of assistance that suit your needs. 
25. If I’m deaf or hard of hearing, can I still join the calls? 
    * We would love to see you there! We offer built-in zoom captions as well as Otter-AI based captioning for realtime calls, and for breakout rooms we split into “written” and “spoken” breakout rooms. If you’d like any other kinds of assistance or accommodations, please let us know. You may also get slightly better-quality captions after the calls, on YouTube, as OLS community call facilitators review the automatic captions and correct errors before uploading the video to the web.

## Microgrants 
26. What’s the maximum amount I can ask for as a microgrant
    * There isn’t a specific number, but the budget is shared amongst all participants in a cohort, including mentors. There’s no need to choose things that are so cheap they end up breaking immediately, but don’t ask for the fanciest new airpods either - try for a sensible middle ground.

    * microgrants@we-are-ols.org reaches the OLS finance team - or try speaking to Paz Bernaldo or Patricia Herterich in Slack. 

## Roles in OLS (mentors/mentees aka project leads/facilitators/experts/speakers) 
27. What mentors do? 
    * Mentors advice and inspire. They help make connections between people, they recommend resources, readings, and share experiences. They provide feedback for mentees to consider. Mentors dedicate around 1 hour per week to the program, but also spend some time before the program to review applications. They will meet their mentees every two weeks during the program and will attend group calls with other mentors to exchange notes and develop their mentoring skills further.
28. What mentors do not do.
    * Mentors are not supervisors. Based on open and reciprocal communication, they are there to guide mentees in the right direction, not “work” on their project. 
29. Who can become a mentor? 
    * Anyone from the OLS community who has previously graduated from the program. Occasionally we look for mentors outside the OLS direct community, and we do so by using our community connections.  When the cohort is about the conclude, we send mentees a survey in which we ask 
30. What do facilitators do? 
    * Together with the Call Hosts or Call Leads, Facilitators allow cohort calls to run smoothly and efficiently. They can have an in-call role, or a post call role (editing the video transcripts and uploading to youtube). This is an invitation-based role. Facilitators are offered an honorarium in recognition of their valuable contributions to the programme
31. Who can become a facilitator? 
    * When the cohort ends we ask participants to complete a survey, and there you can share with us if you’d like to help us facilitate in the next cohort. 
32. Who are Call Leads/Hosts?
    * Generally these are community members who have participated in one or more cohorts in the past, and now are working to pass the skills on wards :) 
33. Who are Experts? 
    * Experts are community members who volunteer part of their time to help other mentees advance their projects. Experts can be contacted by mentors to join one of their mentor-mentee calls and help out with the expertise. You can list yourself as an expert in your own cohort or for future cohorts. 
34. Who are Speakers? 
    * Speakers are those who join cohort calls and give presentations about a certain topic. This is an invitation based role that receives an honorarium. 
35. How does OLS choose speakers? 
    * We look for people who have knowledge and experience in a certain cohort and invite them to join us. They can come from inside the community or from the outside. Once they present in one of the cohort calls they are invited to join our Slack. 
36. Can I suggest/nominate people to be cohort speakers? 
    * You can nominate people to join us as speakers. Use this form to do so: https://forms.gle/aedcPQ9NgpyDgbF16 
37. If I am a mentee, can I volunteer to be a speaker at one of the cohort calls? 
    * Generally, no. We look to distribute opportunities as much as possible. You could certainly join us in a future cohort. 
38. Can I collaborate with OLS on other ideas?
    * Definitely! OLS has a core strategy which helps us decide what initiatives to support - but if you’ve participated in OLS as a mentor, mentee, or expert, you know what kind of missions we care about! Book a chat to discuss more - we’re open to writing grant applications together, supporting you to create a new organisation, partnering with your university, or perhaps something entirely different.

## Mentor-Mentee relationship
49. What do I do if my mentor doesn’t show up to our call? 
    * Contact them first - it could be an honest mistake! If they’re unresponsive, email team@we-are-ols.org or paz@we-are-ols.org or DM Paz and Yo on Slack. We’ll check up with your mentor and make sure they’re doing okay! 
50. What do I do if my mentor doesn’t reply to my emails? 
    * As above - try Slacking them, and if that doesn’t work, contact Paz, Team, or Yo. 
51. What happens if I can’t attend a mentor-mentee meeting? 
    * Let your Mentor know as soon as you can, and try to rearrange for a better time. 
52. Who organises the mentor-mentee meetings? 
    * Coordinate this with your mentor - and make sure to mark your calendar so you don’t miss the meetings. 
53. What to expect and what not to expect from mentors
    * Mentors are here to help you. It’s okay to ask them for feedback on documents, or introductions to someone they know. When you first meet with them, agree how you will communicate. This might be via email or Slack. Don’t expect them to respond out-of-hours - but note that this will vary from mentor to mentor, since OLS is global and for some people their OLS commitments happen in work hours, whilst for others they might prefer out-of-hours meetings.

## Github
57. Do I have to use GitHub?
    * No. We base our training on it as it’s a common and popular collaboration platform, as well as offering a hands-on technical skill that may come in use in the future. But if it doesn’t work for you or your community - go where you’re needed instead. 
58. What about similar platforms like BitBucket, GitLab? 
    * Many features are similar to GitHub. The reason we use GitHub is that it’s one of the most popular tools out there, and we’d like to offer practical, useful skills. Other version control services and collaborations platforms are great too, though! 
59. Who can I ask for help with Github during the cohort?

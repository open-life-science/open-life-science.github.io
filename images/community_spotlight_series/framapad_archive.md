biggest issue is that from this side of the world openness feels like extractivism.

For Emma: How soon did you start 'recruiting' other members after your OLS2 project? how did you decide you needed collaborators (your future committee?
EK - I formed a working group during OLS2 to talk about how to proceed with the work that I had started. The people in this group had been interested when I contacted them about my open science practices research and some of them had already written a paper in my discipline about data sharing practices. The committee was really needed to widen the effort further as we wanted to go for a complete research culture change in our discipline and we knew that open science needs to be applied and thought about differently in different locations - it can't be one size fits all, so we found researchers that could represent all the world regions where our research was happening - this made it 12 members.  
for Emma: How else could you have built the momentum of FAIR Phytoliths, EOSC funding and ICOPS without OLS?
EK - It would have been much slower. The EOSC-life funding, which OLS supported me in applying for,  meant we had two paid people on the project to do alot of the work. This meant we could achieve alot more in shorter time. We then secured more funding for another year to pay the committee to run training for our community. This paid time help to really establish ourselves as a group and in our community that we can now build on that effort.

For Cass: what is the main difference/ advantage of learning skills in community as opposed of 'normal training' in your opinion?
Cass: The training is delivered by people who are close to the problems, so the solutions you are learning about will be a close fit to the problems the community are experiencing
Riva: The learning is also more fluid and reactive based on the experience of delivery within the community. It really shapes the learning experience and outcomes
Cass: Yes! The diversity of the group adds such value to the learning experience.
For Cass: What a beautiful narration! How has your relationship to institutions and collaborations evolved or changed because of the 'kinship' you've made along the way? 
Cass: I'm much more selective about the institutions I work with and I'm currently rebuilding my energy. But it's so important to have a voice in spaces which aren't aligned, as long as you have a way to refill your cup, ideally a community to come back to!
For Lena: What would have had to change for your project to have been a priority internally?
It's about the number of other tasks that were falling on me :) I had to do a lot of operational work to make sure our Research Data Management services are functioning in the most basic sense. We were a very small team. When I was leaving, there was suddenly interest from one of our managers to get this network started. I wrote a detailed plan but no idea if they followed up on it 
For Prash: What barriers have you experienced to being the kind of manager/leader you learnt about in OLS?
Prash: Need to have time to acclimatise to the local context of the project or community
Cass: Exposure to the sort of leadership we have in OLS is essential to build confidence that this is an acceptable (and appropriate!) way to work with a team, with respect, trust, and joy!
